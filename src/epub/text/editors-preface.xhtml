Editor's Preface

The Suspiria have a complex publication history. De Quincey originally published them in Blackwood's Magazine in 1845, intending them to be a kind of sequel to his Confessions of an English Opium-Eater. In their first incarnation, "Dreaming" was presented as an "Introductory Notice", and "Vision of Life" and "Memorial Suspiria" were joined as a single "Part 2".

De Quincey later edited and released an updated version of the Suspiria in his Collected Works. These are the versions of the Suspiria we have included in this edition, with the exception of "The Affliction of Childhood", which he merged into his larger Autobiographic Sketches. We present the initial, independent version of "The Affliction of Childhood" here.

Finally, several Suspiria appeared as posthumous publications, and we include those here as well. We have ordered all of the Suspiria not as they were originally published, but in the order suggested by Alexander H. Japp in his The Posthumous Works of Thomas De Quincey, Volume I.

Of the Suspiria published during De Quincey's life, only the Blackwood's editions have been available as transcriptions online. The edition you're reading now is, to the editor's knowledge, the first transcription of the later Collected Works versions.

"Dreaming," "The Palimpsest of the Human Brain," "Vision of Life," "Memorial Suspiria," "Levana and Our Ladies of Sorrow," and "Savannah-La-Mer" were transcribed from De Quincey's Collected Writings Volume XIII, edited by David Masson.

"Daughter of Lebanon" was transcribed from Volume III of the same set.

"The Apparition of the Brocken" was transcribed from Confessions of an English Opium-Eater, and Kindred Papers.
